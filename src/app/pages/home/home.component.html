F.A.Q

Frequently Asked Questions

  • Finding the Right Nutrient Ratio?

    Once you've assessed your lawn's appearance and diagnosed any specific nutrient deficiencies, it's important to find a fertilizer with the right nutrient ratio. If your lawn looks pretty healthy and you simply want to maintain its current appearance, look for a fertilizer with balanced nutrients. Fertilizer packages have labels with numbers specifying the ratio of each nutrient included in the fertilizer mix. The higher the number relative to other numbers, the more there is of that specific element.

  • Grass Clippings as Fertilizer?

    One important thing to keep in mind is that lawn fertilizers can be harmful to the environment, particularly if you apply so much that it causes runoff. One way to combat this is to sweep up any stray fertilizer granules and toss them back onto your grass rather than in the trash or a compost. You can also help reduce your need for fertilizer by leaving grass clippings on your lawn rather than bagging when you mow. Grass clippings contain nutrients that will feed back into the soil as they break down.

  • Spray Versus Granules?

    Similarly, granules are a better fertilizer type for non-professional lawn care activities. Spray fertilizers have to be applied with much greater regularity than granules, and they can be more difficult to apply evenly. There are some exceptions to this advice, but if you're new to the world of lawn fertilizing, stick with the granules.

  • Quick Versus Slow Release?

    Most homeowners who do their own lawn maintenance will want to use a slow-release fertilizer to avoid having to do too many repeat applications. Quick release formulas can result in burning and other damage to the grass, so it's usually best to leave this type to the pros.
